# 1. Introduction

## 1.1 Background
The gig economy has transformed the nature of work by enabling individuals to provide services on a flexible and on-demand basis. Digital platforms have played a significant role in facilitating interactions between service providers and consumers. Platforms such as Upwork and Airbnb have demonstrated how digital systems can scale service distribution globally (Kässi & Lehdonvirta, 2018; Sundararajan, 2016).

Despite these advancements, most gig economy platforms operate under a two-sided interaction model that focuses primarily on transactional exchanges. This approach is effective for discrete tasks but insufficient for activity-based services such as coaching, workshops, and community programs, which require structured coordination, scheduling, and recurring engagement.

Additionally, identity representation in existing platforms remains largely self-declared. Users present profiles, skills, and experiences that may not be fully verifiable, creating a gap between perceived identity and actual participation. This limitation affects trust, progression tracking, and long-term ecosystem development.

## 1.2 Problem Statement
Existing gig economy platforms face several interconnected limitations. First, most platforms do not integrate operational roles into their interaction models, even though coordination actors are often essential in activity-based services. Second, they provide limited support for structured and recurring activities, which makes them less suitable for engagements that unfold over time rather than in a single transaction. Third, their identity systems are still largely based on self-declared information, reducing the reliability of user representation. Finally, they rarely offer mechanisms to track progression and actual participation in a way that can support long-term trust and ecosystem development.

## 1.3 Research Objective
This study aims to propose a triadic interaction model that integrates providers, consumers, and operators within a single framework. It also positions activity as the fundamental unit of interaction, develops an activity-verified identity system, and supports scalable participation across multiple domains.

## 1.4 Contribution
The main contributions of this paper are a triadic interaction framework for gig economy systems, an activity-verified identity model, a multi-domain interaction approach, and a conceptual architecture designed to support scalable growth.
